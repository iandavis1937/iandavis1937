📊 What is a Histogram?

A histogram uses bars to show how often values fall in each interval. The x-axis shows intervals (called bins), the y-axis shows how many data points fall in each bin, and the height of each bar represents the frequency for that bin.

📦 What are Bins?

Bins (or class intervals) are groups that organize continuous data. This game uses 3-year-wide bins: "14–16" contains ages 14, 15, and 16; "17–19" contains 17, 18, and 19; and so on. Counting how many data points fall in each bin gives us the frequency.

📐 Distribution Shapes

Skewed right (positively skewed): tail extends to the right; most data on the left.
Skewed left: tail extends left; most data on the right.
Bell-shaped (symmetric): roughly equal on both sides, peaked in the middle.

Statistics Insight: What Do These Distributions Tell Us?

These six sports illustrate how physical demands shape an athlete's career arc. Women's Artistic Gymnastics peaks extremely young (17–19) because it rewards explosive power, flexibility, and fearlessness that are often at their apex before full skeletal maturation. The distribution is sharply right-skewed, with almost no athletes past age 23.

Men's 200m Freestyle Swimming peaks slightly later (21–23), combining raw speed (youth) with technical refinement (experience), producing a distribution that is fairly bell-shaped but still skewed right.

Men's Weightlifting peaks in the late 20s (26–28). Building elite-level strength and perfecting the snatch and clean-and-jerk technique requires years, so the distribution is roughly symmetric and centered later than either aquatic sport.

Equestrian Dressage stands apart with a distribution spanning ages 30–52 and peaking around 38–40. The sport rewards the deep partnership between rider and horse — a skill that only grows with decades of practice. Its distribution is skewed left with an unusually wide spread.

Olympic Sailing has no dominant peak — athletes compete successfully from their early 20s all the way into their mid-40s. The distribution is roughly uniform, reflecting a sport where tactical intelligence and experience offset the physical advantages of youth, so no single age range dominates.

Olympic Trap Shooting is the clearest example of a left-skewed distribution. The peak sits at 44–46 — unusually late for any sport — and the distribution falls off steeply to the right while trailing back to age 30 on the left. Because accuracy and calm focus take 15–20 years of competitive repetition to develop, youth is almost no advantage at all.

Understanding distribution shapes helps statisticians and sports scientists predict when athletes are most likely to perform at their best — and design training programs accordingly.
